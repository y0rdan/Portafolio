# T1566.001 — Phishing: Office Document Spawning Shell Process

**Tactic:** Initial Access  
**Severity:** High  
**Data Source:** `DeviceProcessEvents` (Defender for Endpoint)  
**Query Frequency:** Every 1 hour | **Lookback:** 24 hours

---

## Detection Rationale

Malicious Office macros commonly spawn cmd.exe, PowerShell, wscript, or mshta as child processes to execute payloads delivered via phishing emails. Legitimate Office use rarely produces these child process relationships, making this a high-signal detection when filtered against approved automation.

---

## KQL Query

```kql
DeviceProcessEvents
| where TimeGenerated > ago(1d)
| where InitiatingProcessFileName in~ ("winword.exe","excel.exe","powerpnt.exe","outlook.exe")
| where FileName in~ ("cmd.exe","powershell.exe","wscript.exe","cscript.exe","mshta.exe","rundll32.exe")
| project TimeGenerated, DeviceName, AccountName,
    InitiatingProcessFileName, FileName, ProcessCommandLine
```

**Exclusions Applied:**
- Excluded approved macro-enabled templates by file hash (finance team)
- Excluded finance team devices for approved Excel automation workflows
- Excluded IT-signed scripts launched via Outlook rules

**Analyst Notes:** Any unsigned or externally-received document spawning a shell process is critical. Always check email source headers and whether the document arrived from outside the organization.

---

## Response Actions

1. Identify the Office document that triggered the spawn — was it received via email?
2. Check sender and whether the email passed SPF/DKIM/DMARC
3. Quarantine the email from all mailboxes if confirmed malicious (Defender for Office 365)
4. Isolate the endpoint
5. Decode and analyze the spawned command for payload, C2 URLs, or persistence mechanisms
